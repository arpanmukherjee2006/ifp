Empowering women farmers through accessible lac cultivation on Flemingia host plants

Lac, the natural resin secreted by the lac insect (Kerria lacca), has long been a source of livelihood for tribal communities in Jharkhand. Traditionally, it is cultivated on Kusum, Palash, and Ber trees. However, lac farming on these host trees often faces challenges such as large tree size, difficult management practices, and the migration of male workers. These constraints have led to reduced production over time.

Alternate Host Plants

Flemingia semialata & Flemingia macrophylla

Today, Flemingia semialata and Flemingia macrophylla, small and easily manageable shrubs, have emerged as promising alternate host plants for lac cultivation. Their short stature, simple handling, and early harvest make lac farming more accessible, particularly for women farmers, while also supporting the premium Kusumi strain of lac insect to ensure superior quality and higher productivity.

Agroforestry Integration

Enhanced Profitability through Intercropping

In addition, intercropping within agroforestry models can significantly enhance overall profitability. Models integrating vegetables such as cabbage, cauliflower, and chili, fruits like papaya, and flowers such as marigold have already been developed by ICFRE-IFP, and further improved varieties are currently in the pipeline.

Impact & Future

Empowering Communities

This innovative approach to lac cultivation represents a significant step forward in making sustainable livelihoods more accessible to women farmers and tribal communities in Jharkhand. By combining traditional knowledge with modern agricultural practices, ICFRE-IFP is paving the way for enhanced productivity, improved quality, and greater economic empowerment in rural areas.
